# PMO AI Tool — How the Workflows Work

A plain-English guide to what the AI does, why it works that way, and what value it delivers.

---

## Overview

The PMO AI tool focuses on four areas where PMOs spend the most time on repetitive, judgment-heavy work:

| # | Work Stream | The Problem It Solves |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Meeting Intelligence** | Action items and decisions get lost after meetings |
| 2 | **Status Reports** | Pulling data and writing narratives takes hours every week |
| 3 | **Strategic Resourcing** | Resource gaps are spotted too late to act on |
| 4 | **Personal Task Management** | Commitments scattered across emails, meetings, and JIRA get missed |

> **One rule applies across all four:** The AI proposes, humans approve. Nothing gets written to a system or sent externally without a PMO sign-off.

---

## Work Stream 1: Meeting Intelligence

*"No more lost action items. No more 'I thought we decided X.'"*

This is the highest-priority stream — it affects every meeting, every day.

### The Problem

After a meeting, a PMO typically spends 20–30 minutes:
- Re-reading notes to find action items
- Guessing who owns what
- Deciding what is a Risk vs an Issue vs a Decision
- Manually updating JIRA and the RAID log
- Writing and sending a follow-up email

This happens ~80 times a month. Items get missed. Decisions get disputed.

---

### How It Works — Step by Step

#### Step 1: Ingest the transcript
The AI reads the meeting transcript from Teams or Zoom. It identifies speakers, cleans up the text, and links the meeting to the relevant project.

**Why this way:** Transcripts are already generated by Teams/Zoom — there is no extra effort from the PMO. The AI just picks it up automatically.

**Value:** Zero manual input needed to start the process.

---

#### Step 2: Generate a meeting summary
The AI produces a short, structured summary — key topics discussed, main points, next steps — written for the audience (e.g. more detailed for the project team, higher-level for an executive).

**Why this way:** A PMO should not spend time writing up what happened in a meeting they just sat through. The AI drafts it; the PMO refines if needed.

**Value:** 20–30 minutes of note-writing eliminated per meeting.

---

#### Step 3: Extract RAID items
This is the most important step. The AI reads the transcript and identifies four types of items:

| Type | What It Looks For | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Risk (R)** | Future problems that might happen | *"We might miss the deadline if the vendor is late"* |
| **Action (A)** | Tasks someone committed to doing | *"John will send the updated plan by Friday"* |
| **Issue (I)** | Problems happening right now | *"The integration is broken and blocking testing"* |
| **Decision (D)** | Things that were agreed | *"We agreed to use Vendor B"* |

For each item it finds, the AI assigns a **confidence score**. Anything below 80% confidence is automatically flagged for the PMO to review.

**Why this way:** These distinctions require judgment — a human reader's understanding, not just keyword matching. AI is well-suited to this but still needs oversight, especially for Decisions and Risks where getting it wrong matters most.

**Value:** RAID items that currently get lost are captured consistently. Decisions are documented, preventing costly "I thought we agreed X" disputes later.

---

#### Step 4: Assign owners and due dates
The AI identifies who is responsible for each action item, and converts vague timelines into real dates (e.g. *"by end of week"* → a specific date).

**Why this way:** Ownership and dates are often implied rather than explicit in conversation. The AI infers these from context, but flags anything ambiguous for human confirmation.

**Value:** Action items arrive with owners and dates already filled in, not as a list of vague statements.

---

#### Step 5: Propose JIRA updates
The AI matches action items to existing JIRA tickets and prepares updates — new comments, status changes, or new tickets where needed. High-priority ticket changes are always held for human approval.

**Why this way:** The PMO would otherwise do this manually by cross-referencing the meeting notes against the JIRA board. The AI does the matching work; the PMO just confirms.

**Value:** JIRA stays current after every meeting without the PMO having to manually update it.

---

#### Step 6: Propose RAID log entries
The AI formats all extracted items into the RAID log schema and queues them for PMO approval. **Nothing is written to the log until a human approves it.**

**Why this way:** The RAID log is a governance document — it can be audited and used as evidence of decisions made. It must be accurate and human-verified.

**Value:** The RAID log gets updated after every meeting, not just when someone remembers. All entries are traceable back to the exact moment in the meeting transcript.

---

#### Step 7: Draft the follow-up email
The AI writes a follow-up email listing RAID items, owners, due dates, and key decisions, ready to send to attendees. The PMO reviews and sends it — the AI never sends independently.

**Why this way:** Follow-up emails represent the organisation externally. They need a human check before they go out.

**Value:** A professional, complete follow-up email ready in under 30 minutes of the meeting ending, instead of hours later (or not at all).

## Work Stream 2: Status Reports & Scorecards

*"Stop spending a day a week pulling numbers and writing the same report."*

### The Problem

PMOs spend significant time each week:
- Collecting project data from JIRA, SharePoint, and MS Project
- Calculating RAG (Red/Amber/Green) scores
- Writing the narrative that explains what the numbers mean
- Formatting and distributing reports to different audiences

This is repetitive, but the *interpretation* requires real judgment.

---

### How It Works

| Step | What the AI Does | What the PMO Does |
|---|---|---|
| **Data collection** | Pulls metrics automatically from JIRA, SharePoint, MS Project via API | Nothing — fully automated |
| **RAG calculation** | Applies the formula to produce Red/Amber/Green scores | Reviews the output |
| **Reconciliation** | Flags inconsistencies across data sources | Resolves conflicts |
| **Narrative writing** | Drafts the "so what" story behind the numbers | Reviews and refines |
| **Executive summary** | Tailors the narrative for senior audience | Approves before sharing |
| **Distribution** | Sends the report to the right people | Nothing — fully automated once approved |

**Why this way:** Data collection and formatting are pure automation — no judgment needed. Writing the narrative requires understanding context, priorities, and audience — that is where the AI assists rather than replaces.

**Value:** A report that currently takes 45 minutes to produce takes 10. The PMO spends time on interpretation and decisions, not data wrangling.

## Work Stream 3: Strategic Resourcing

*"Know about resource gaps before they become a crisis."*

### The Problem

PMOs often find out a resource is finishing a project too late to find a replacement. Matching the right person to a role requires knowing availability, skills, and project context — information that lives in different places.

---

### How It Works

| Step | What the AI Does | What the PMO Does |
|---|---|---|
| **Resource tracking** | Monitors project end dates and flags resources finishing soon | Reviews alerts |
| **Skills inventory** | Maintains an up-to-date view of skills across the pool | Validates accuracy |
| **Gap detection** | Identifies upcoming gaps before they happen | Prioritises which gaps to act on |
| **Match recommendation** | Suggests the best-fit resource with trade-off explanation | Makes the final call |
| **Resource commitment** | N/A — humans only | Confirms the assignment |

**Why this way:** Tracking and inventory are simple data operations — automation is reliable here. Matching involves trade-offs (availability vs skill fit vs project priority) that need human judgement. Committing a person to a project is always a human decision.

**Value:** Resource gaps surface weeks earlier, when there is still time to act. The PMO spends time on decisions, not chasing spreadsheets.

## Work Stream 4: Personal Task Management

*"One place for everything you have committed to doing."*

### The Problem

A PMO's commitments are scattered across:
- Emails ("I'll get that to you by Thursday")
- Meeting action items
- JIRA tickets
- Slack/Teams messages

Without a consolidated view, things get missed.

---

### How It Works

| Step | What the AI Does | What the PMO Does |
|---|---|---|
| **Email scanning** | Reads the last 3 days of emails and extracts commitments made or received | Reviews the list |
| **Cross-source consolidation** | Combines tasks from email, meetings, and JIRA into one list, removing duplicates | Spot-checks |
| **Prioritisation** | Ranks by urgency, requester importance, and project priority; surfaces overdue items | Adjusts if needed |
| **Completion detection** | Monitors sent folder and JIRA changes to automatically tick off completed tasks | Confirms if uncertain |
| **Reminder generation** | Sends reminders at the right time via the preferred channel (email/Teams) | None |
| **Checklist interface** | Presents a clean daily task list; allows manual tick-off | Works through the list |

**Why this way:** Extracting and consolidating tasks is mechanical but requires reading comprehension to distinguish *"I'll try"* (soft commitment) from *"I will send it Friday"* (hard commitment). The AI is conservative — when in doubt, it flags rather than assumes.

**Value:** Nothing slips through. One daily list replaces inbox scanning, meeting note re-reading, and JIRA board checking. Estimated time saving: up to 2 hours per day.

## Summary: What Changes for a PMO

| Today | With the AI Tool |
|---|---|
| 30 min writing up meeting notes | Draft ready automatically |
| RAID items lost or forgotten | Captured from every meeting, in the log within 30 min |
| JIRA updated manually (or not at all) | Updates proposed automatically after each meeting |
| Status reports take 45 min each | Takes 10 min to review and approve |
| Resource gaps found too late | Flagged weeks in advance |
| Commitments missed across email/meetings | One consolidated daily task list |

**The AI handles the repetitive work. The PMO retains control over everything that matters.**
